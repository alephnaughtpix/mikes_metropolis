  1. Summary of common words used in the computer scene
     
    • CD- if used in the context of a floppy-disk, it means "compacted disk"- that is, a floppy disk containing many program compacted using a packer.
    • coder- demo programmer
    • Cracker- Person who removes copy-protection form programs.
    • Crew- Scene group
    • Demo- Demonstration of group's work
    • Dentro- Single program demo
    • Diskmag- Interactive scene magazine on disk.
    • Graphist- computer graphics artist
    • Greetings- Crews saying "hello" to one another.
    • Intro- Introduction demo screen
    • Megademo- A collection of many demos.
    • Module- a multi-channel piece of music made with sampled instruments.
    • Packer- program which packs binary data. This is different from an archiver in the sense that the compacted program often can be set to unpack itself and run without the need for an unpacking program, and that source code for unpacking packed data is included to allow other programmers to use it within their programs.
    • Screen- Single demo
    • Scroller- A continuous moving line of text on a screen.The text being moved usally called a scrolltext. Usually contains info about the demo, grettings to other demo crews, what the person writing the scrolltext had for breakfast, etc., etc., .....
    • Swapper- Swaps scene programs with other swappers.
    • Trackloader- floppy disk based demo

    A more detailed (and humorous) list of definitions can be found at http://grelb.src.gla.ac.uk:8000/~mjames/pb/words.html

  2. INTRODUCTION
     
    1. What is the home computer "scene"?

        2. It's a term for a whole load of people using home computers (Mostly Ataris, Amigas and PCs) for completely computing-related recreational pursuits.

    2. How is it different from games playing, or using computers for a hobby?

        3. Not, not exactly. Those sort of people certainly use computers, but as a means to an end, whether it be as a "productivity tool" or as a means to entertainment. However, with the computer scene, the computer is an end in itself- all the hobbies mentioned below are not possible in any form without computers, and they usually change in style dramatically according to which computer you are on.

    3. How do you use computers for computers sake?

        4. Mainly through programming for fun! This usually takes the form of demos, though there are a couple of other avenues....

  3. INTRODUCTION TO DEMOS
     
    1. What is a demo?

        2. A demo is generally a program done by a group of people, or demo crew, to show off what they can do on a particular machine. (Hence "demo", short for "demonstration".)

    2. What can you expect in the average demo?

        3. Though this depends on what machine you are running a demo, there are general similarities:

      1. Graphics
        Usually done by the demo crews graphics artist, if they have one. There is great demand for good graphics artists because a good looking demo gets spread widely (Especially in the case of Amiga demos). Graphics in demos doesn't just cover the area of pictures, it also includes things such as fonts, colours, etc.,

        2. Until recently, the most impressive graphics have been on Amiga demos, however, now the Atari Falcon and recent PC video cards are bringing the potential for impressive graphics on these machines.

      2. Sound
        Most big demo crews also have their own musician, who provides music to accompany the demos. (No demo is taken seriously these days if it doesn't have some form of soundtrack.) Until recently there was a major difference between the Amiga style of music, which used it's advanced soundchip to play multi-channel modules played using sampled instruments, and the Atari style, which played "bleepy" music (And the odd sample, or even an Amiga module, using lots of careful programming) using a far inferior soundchip. However, with the improved sound in the Atari STE, the Digital Signal Processor in the Atari Falcon, and the advanced technology of PC soundcards, the Amiga style of module is becoming the accepted standard in demo music, though the Screamtracker (S3M) format is also gaining acceptance on the PC.

      3. Effects
        This the most important part of any demo. Usually the idea is to make effects which have either not been seen before, or look improbable given the hardware and work required (eg "They said fullscreen couldn't be done on an ST, but look at this...") There are lots of classic demo effects, listed later

      4. Greetings
        This is the other important part of the demo, where demo crews say hello to everyone who's work they like, and also greet back demo crews who have greeted them in their demos. 
         
  4. What do these demo crews get out of it?

      5. Oh come on, this is a hobby. Why does it have to have a purpose?

  5. ORIGIN OF DEMOS

    6. Note- Any extra info here would be appreciated!

    1. The Early 80s- birth of the 8-bit scene

      2. Though people have been doing "display" programs for as long as computers have existed, the earliest examples of programs recognisable as "demos" date from the 8 bit home computer era, when (then) state of the art graphics and sound were available at a comparitively cheap price, and the idea of an "all-in-one" computer architecture was developed (Both at around the same time by Sinclair Research and Acorn computers, though there's a bit of dispute about who was actually first.) allowing the programmer to control the hardware from the main processor.

      The main home computers (At least in Europe) were the Sinclair ZX Spectrum, the Acorn BBC Micro, and the Commodore 64. All three had a massive fanbase, and major print magazines, which usually carried listings if programs to type in- thus giving many their first experience of programming languages.

      The Spectrum was an immensely popular machine for games, and had a very thorough hardware reference section in it's user manual, allowing users to exploit the machines hardware to the full. The BBC Micro was a popular machine with educational establishments due to it's tie-in with the BBC's Computer Literacy programme, it had one of the best built-in versions of BASIC around at the time, and was highly regarded in the sound and graphics department- this was the platform on which "Elite", the ground-breaking 3D graphics trading/dogfight game, was first launched.

      However, the Commodore 64, a comparatively late entry to the 8-bit home computer scene, had even more advanced hardware for the time- the graphics handling included hardware sprites. The soundchip (SID) was even more famous for it's then arcade quality sound. Music for C64 games started to have a recognisable quality that couldn't be bettered by the other 8-bits (Although they did try), and some C64 musicians even began to create a name for themselves with their recognisable sound. (Most famously Rob Hubbard) The SID chip could even, with a bit of careful programming play sampled sound, a trick which was very quickly copied on the BBC and Speccy. The origins of music in today's demos can be traced back to the SID music on C64 games.

      Just as important was the various "fastload" systems on the C64. Back in the days when home computers loaded programs from cassette tape systems, it could take quite a few minutes! (The Internet's not much quicker these days, is it?) Unlike the BBC and Speccy, which could load programs from any cassette player hooked up to them, the C64 had it's own special tape player. Using careful programming, some software companies managed to write and read data to the cassettes at much faster rates, thus allowing programs to be loaded much faster from tape. (It was also used as a form of protection.) Initially these fastload systems distinguished themselves from the normal loading method by constantly changing the background colour. (A lot of packers on 16-bit computers also did this.) Then, some of them became a bit more artistic, and started using sequences of colours that were pleasing to the eye. Then, there came the fastloaders with the initial opening picture. Then, a couple of games started adding in music with the picture (eg Rambo, with excellent music by Rob Hubbard), and then.... Novaload!

      Probably the tackiest fastload system of the lot, US Gold's Novaload system was first used on the Cold War scenario game "Raid Over Moscow", the object of which is to single-handedly fly a spaceship from a Star Wars space-station, knock out the entire defenses of The Kremlin, and blow up Moscow in order to avert a nuclear attack on the US!!!! If you though that was a bit unsubtle, you should have seen the Novaload system which loaded the game! First you would see the title "Novaload" and the version number, accompanied by what sounded like the sound output of the tape player fed through the sound output. Then suddenly, the US flag and US Gold logo filled the screen, accompanied by "The Star Spangled Banner"! Also in the screen was a loading block countdown, and a scroller advertising various US Gold releases. All this was happening while the program was being fastloaded. (Oh yes, and it also played "The Sash" and "Yanky Doodle Dandy".) Music, graphics, and a scroller- could this be an early example of a demo?

      It must also be mentioned that there was a pirate scene on 8-bit computers, and often pirates added simple intros before the games, usually including something like a small scroller saying something like "Jolly Bug proudly presents Chuckie Egg- the Director's Cut", or even a small picture or a little bit of music. However, the complexity of these small "demos" was restricted not only by the hardware of the computer, but the fact that it, like all games at the time, would have to be loaded from tape, which is a little bit slow!!

      However, more recently, with the relative cheapness of disk drives, both the C64 and Spectrum have established a thriving demo scene, which exists to this very day!

  6. TYPES OF DEMOS
    • Demo / screen
      This usually suggests just one screen of effects.
    • Megademo
      A whole load of screens, linked together.
    • Intro
      A demo screen which loads before anything else- usually meant as an introduction to either a demo, or another scene-type program.
    • Dentro
      A single demo, like an intro, but on it's own. A bit like a demo, but it's usually much shorter, and is often used as a preview to a megademo.
    • Trackmo / Trackloader
      This refers to a floppy disk-based demo/dentro.
    • Hello-tro
      A debut dentro/demo done by a new demo crew.
    • Menu screen
      This is a screen, mostly appearing in megademos, particularly on the ST, which allows you to choose an individual demo screen to view.
    • Rolling / Amiga-style / Sequential loader demo
      This is a megademo, without the menu, which runs all the screens in a predetermined order, often carefully timed.
