Demos I have personally been involved in.

  • Perpetual Dawn Demo. (Atari STFM/STE)- Although not officially by me, some of the code (The fading routines and the sample player) is my code! (Anyway, it's about the Ripped Off Coding Party I was at in September 1992, and it's also the debut of one EGB's Smelly Sox, who went on to edit PB Diskmag. However my first proper demo was on....
  • Pete (Ripped Off Party Demo).- (Atari STFM/STE) Check out my not-very-good demo screen for Blue Harvest! I set up Blue Harvest as a demo group with this guy that was supposed to be the musician (Who also supplied the name.), but he didn't do anything, so I had to use ripped music here, and considering that I had to do everything else in the screen, I was a bit pissed off to say the least! However, it did spur me onto trying my hand at doing chip music, and through that I learned how to make Protracker music, which was first heard on....
  • The Jaguar Demo. (Atari Falcon)- I did the music for this one. (Under my Amiga "Metronome of Adrenalin" pseudonym.) Also, <Genie!> converted my fade routine from the Perpetual Dawn Demo to work on Truecolour pixels, thus generating the brilliant sideways fade between pictures that everyone thought was so clever at the time.
  • The 3.5K Megademo. - (Atari STFM/STE)- I was asked to do a screen for PB Diskmag, and got the idea of doing a menu compilation of 3.5K Demos from the ST News Coding Conference 2 as a single program that could "load" and run the 3.5K demos in a megademo style, and wouldn't need to be reloaded. This meant hacking into the original programs as most of them didn't exit back properly, and some used the system! The menu contains one of my little heard chip music compilations! (It wasn't released in PB though...)
  • All demos by the Senior Dads! - Look, you don't really need details on this legend, do you? OK then, the first demo Anal Tuck (Atari Falcon 030) was, as documented in Maggie, very hurriedly put together 30 minutes before the deadline for the Fried Bits 3 96Ktros Competition, and as luck would have it, it was the last of the demos in the competition to be shown to voters, and was shown right after Inter's Terrorise Your Soul. The contrast between Inter's brute force coding and pyrotechnics of precalculation, and Senior Dads simple and casual re-writing of the demo rule book, combined with the fact that it was the last demo, struck a chord with the voters, and Anal Tuck consquently got 5th place! (Not 6th as you might have read in DBA magazine. Were you handed the same stats sheet as me, Slimer?)

    • The revolutionary "water" technique of the next demo Colonic Irrigation began right after that fateful demo competition, but wasn't completed until a few months later, complete with the unique innovation of the Phase Four Stereo soundtrack. The rest is history, and I can't be bothered writing about it...

I've also been involved with one or two disk magazines as well, if you're interested.
