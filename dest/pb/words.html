"What the hell are you talking about?"

AT LAST- PB DISKMAG EXPLAINS ALL THOSE FUNNY WORDS!

If this is your first introduction to the amazing world of the underground computer scene, then you might be initially confused by some of the strange and varied terms that might be used. Some of them are used because they describe unique concepts and ideas, and others are used by people who think they are important, and just want to be incredibly awkward to us mere mortals! But don't worry, we want you to enjoy reading this magazine, so it's in our interest for our readers to understand what the hell is going on! So, for your rough guide to underground terminology, read on!

COMPUTER Er, OK then, I'll be a bit more adventurous then, shall I?
ATARI This was one of the main 16-bit home computer ranges in Europe. Atari manufactured the STFM, the STE, the Falcon 030, and the TT. The ST in particular was very popular, and had a massive scene. It was also popular with musicians because of it's relative cheapness and built in MIDI ports, and was the original platform on which the MIDI sequencing programs Cubase and Notator appeared. Most of the underground Atari scene is now on the Falcon, having moved en massed from the ST and STE. However, there has recently been a mini-revival of "retro" coding on the 16-bits, and many Eastern European countries (in particular Poland) are starting to develop an ST scene.
AMIGA The other main 16-bit computer range originally made by Commodore, very popular in Europe and UK, and the US, it introduced pre-emptive multitasking into home computers. Mainly popular for it's games, it's graphics applications (eg Newtek's Video Toaster, used by the sci-fi TV show "Babylon Five"), and it's sampled sound applications (eg Octamed, and the original Soundtracker.) Although the hardware is a bit more difficult to program than on the ST, it's very versatile, and a major underground Amiga scene developed. Like the Atari scene, the main Amiga scene has moved to the newer computers, in this case the A1200, from the older A500/A500+. However, again there is a mini "retro" A500 revival gathering pace.
PC There are many people who think PC just means "computer". Well, yes, it's literal meaning is "personal computer". However, PC as a platform refers to a particularly crappy type of home computer based on the old IBM Personal Computer range which has become the de-facto market leader in home computers. There are almost as many companies making PC parts as there are people owning them. The actual system architecture is horrendously outdated, as shown by the fact that every PC owner is expected to keep forking out extra money for sound and graphics upgrades if they want to play games on them! And as for Windows 95- don't get us started. You need at least 8 megs of memory and a couple hundred megabytes of hard-drive space plus 486 at least if you want to do a poor version of pre-emptive multitasking that even a 1984 half megabyte floppy-disk based Amiga system could achieve. If you are reading this on a PC (Which you probably are), let me tell you (if you don't know already) .... Your computer's crap!
CONSOLES Do not confuse with computers- these are specifically engineered just to play games! Only worth mentioning for the fact that a large underground scene has resulted from people who already have home computers linking them up to consoles. Originally it was to pirate games, but now they're doing computery things with them! The final irony is that Sega has recently released a version of the Saturn to allow PC users to program for it!
DISK Even in these days of hard drives and CD-ROMs, I'm pretty sure most readers will know what a disk is, but just in case any console owners are reading this, it's like a smaller games cartridge, (Ha ha!) except you can save computer information on it!
DISKMAG A diskmag is a magazine on a disk ie like a magazine only like it's on a disk, a bit like PB for example! Diskmags started with the 16-bit computer scene, when 3.5" floppys were first introduced as the standard method of exchanges of data. An important part of any computer scene.
CD-ROM This is a storage format which uses CDs, much like the ones for Hi-fi's, but stores computer information rather than sound information. At the moment, it's not as popular as disks for information exchange because it's a bit expensive, especially writeable CD-ROM drives, however most PC software these days is being released on CD-ROM.
PROCESSOR The bit in the computer that processes things! So fuckin' what? Well, it runs programs like our diskmag and the computers that we edit this magazine with, so we think it's fuckin' important! Atari and Amiga computers use Motorola processors, like the 68000 in the ST/STE and A500, the 68020 (Nice!) in the A1200, or the 68030 (Very nice!) in the Falcon. PCs use 8086 series and Pentiums, both of which, as everyone knows, are shite! (Well, if you didn't know that already, we're telling you now, and that's from actual experience of programming in assembler!)
DSP This is a co-processor, which means that it works seperately from the main processor in a computer. The DSP stands for Digital Signal Processor, so as you can guess it's pretty good for digital sound processing, but it's also good for other mathematical stuff as well. You hear a lot about these in digital sound equipment, and many PC soundcards have them. However, there are also programmable DSPs, like the one in the Atari Falcon 030- it's another Motorola job, and it's full name is DSP56001!
COPPER A lot of PC users might have heard of "copper bars" in demos, but don't know the origin of this phrase! Well, it comes from the Amiga Copper chip. Short for co-processor, this chip allows you to change various values of hardware in the Amiga's hardware cocurrent with (Almost) any position of the video beam. For some strange no reason, no computer has yet to anything like this (Although this may change when Phase 5's A/Box project is released.) Why, we don't know, because you can do impressive things like changing display memory halfway down a screen (eg for a two-player split screen game) without having to re-draw the entire screen, or change the colour every scanline for a gradiated colour background, all without any overhead on the main processor! Early Amiga demos used to the colour chnging trick, creating horizontal bar shapes which became known as, yup, "copper bars"! Even wierder, demo coders are now programming the copper to do a 24-bit true-colour display!
PROGRAM OK, this is only just in case you haven't heard of this concept, (eg All you people who think in terms of "applications" and "packages".) but a program is a load of computer instructions set by the user! In this case, we as the users did a whole load of instructions, which resulted in our amazing diskmag!!
SOFTWARE Another word for program, it's usually used in reference with commercially sold programs. There's also another type of software called "pirate software" ie commercial software that's been copied!
FAST Originally FAST (Federation Against Software Theft.) was a coalition, made of software industry people who didn't like the idea of pirate software! They get involved a lot in "raids" against sellers of pirate software, most of which were not very successful. FAST have now been disbanded now, and are replaced by a "task force" set up by the European Lesiure Software Publishers Association (ELSPA), and also concentrate on the more headline-grabbing issues of computer porn. Many major software companies (Microsoft, for example) also have their own staff to deal with piracy of their own software.
BARRAS Or "Barrowlands Market". This is a very large street market (Spread over quite a few streets, actually) in Glasgow, Scotland, and it contains quite a lot of stalls, and a few of them are selling pirate software! Though, to be fair, there are one or two excellent PD stalls, and we really think you should pay them a visit. What do you mean What's PD??!?!?!
PUBLIC DOMAIN (Or PD for short.) This is the phrase given to non- copyrighted software, which can be freely copied and spread as far as possible, like for example, our amazing diskmag! Nearly all "scene" stuff is in the public domain. You usually need to have an "ear to the ground" to hear about latest developments in this area, in whcih case, you're currently browsing in the right area here! Quite a few companies have sprung up selling this sort of stuff. We don't mind this too much, as long as they don't make any lofty pronouncements about why we should change our programs in order that they can get better sales! Many scene programs come with a disclaimer asking PD companies who sell the program to either register with them and/or send the a donation.
SHAREWARE This is like PD, only if you find the program useful, then you're expected to send some money to the programmer! You usually get extras, like for example a more complete version of the program, or one that works beyond 30 days, if you register with the author of the program, and it usually means you don't have to suffer crappy messages along the lines of "please please register with me!". Some, of them, or course are worth every penny. Famous examples of shareware include the PC games Doom, Duke Nukem, and Quake.
LICENCEWARE Like PD again, but although it can be copied freely, you're not allowed to sell the copies for profit unless you have a licence from the people who publish the software! The software is usually licenced by a company, such as the old Atari company Budgie UK, to a few registered PD companies.
GEM That funny "desktop" that comes up when you switch on an Atari computer! It's a graphical user interface that allows you to do various things like copying files, running programs, and so on, by merely acting on graphical representations of objects using the mouse. Originally it started life as an business application on the PC, and it looked shit! It improved amazingly by the time it appeared as a desktop on the Atari ST, and approached the level of really good by the time the Falcon came along. One good thing about Atari Gem is that it's in ROM, so you don't need to load it off a disk every time you want to use it. This meant the ST was a pretty good system even without a hard drive!
WORKBENCH Equivalent of GEM on the Amiga. A little bit fiddly to use because of it's use of pre-emptive multitasking. (Even if you've used Windows 95, you might be surprised at how "open-plan" and hyper-responsive the standard Workbench set-up is in use.) However, it's also a good system, even better when it's properly configured and extra bolt-ons are added. Unlike Atari GEM, it has to be loaded from disk, so it's a good idea to have a hard disk!
MULTITOS The "normal" GEM desktop doesn't have pre-emptive multitasking. However, MultiTos adds this feature, as well as further improvments in GEM. Of course, if any of the programs assume that they're the only ones running, (ie like many old Atari programs, hog all the memory so that the others don't have any) then the others are fucked, and it becomes a single-tasking system again- or if the remaining program itself doesn't like MultiTos and crashes, a no-tasking system!!!
GRAPHICS Computer pictures! (Easy one there, thank God!)
GFX Short for graphics.
3D/TRIDI This is a term for graphics which use a 3D coordinate system. (For example, "Frontier/Elite 2" and "Doom" use 3D graphics.)
CHIP MUSIC Music made using a crappy sound generator that is present in all Atari computers, and is the main soundchip in STs, worse luck! Needless to say, it sounds pretty shite! There have been good chip musicians like Mad Max, Count Zero, Jess of OVR, who have stretched the chip to it's limits, but trying to make bleepy stuff sound really good is a bit like trying to do a heavy metal song with a couple of plastic banjos! There is also an equivalent type of music in Amigas, though why people bother when they can use the Amigas custom hardware to play sampled sound, is anyones guess! The ST sound chip can play samples with some clever programming, but it uses a lot of processor time.
MUZIK Another word for chip-music, usually used by complete morons.
MUZAK Yet another one.
MUZAX Guess what?!?!?
ZIK This one's used by complete morons who use big letters!
ZAX Oh bloody hell, this doesn't even have any of the letters of the word it's supposed to represent.
DIGI DRUMS A newer form of chip music on the ST, where the drum sounds are actually sampled instruments, though for some reason, they always seem to sound like someone spitting down a plastic drainpipe!
SID MUSIC An emulatation of the authentic "buzztone" sound of the long-departed Commodore 64 home computers "SID" sound-chip! Using special programming, your computer can sound like a very musical wasp put through a phaser!
MODULES Multi-track music made using sampled instruments. This is a format which originated on the Amiga. It sounds better than chip music, but it's much harder to do on an ST. The STE and Falcon have a much better sound system, so there should be no excuse for not playing modules on these computers. This format has also crossed to the PC and other computers- they're even playing modules on Sinclair Spectrums these days!!!
MODS Someone who in the early 60s fought the Rockers on Brighton Beach. Also short for module, fuckwit!
CODER Someone who does a lot of code! Only in this case, the code is assembly code (or if you're on the PC, C as well!), the basic language that makes the processor in a computer go, and the sort of language the menu interface of our amazing diskmag is written in!
GRAPHIST Or graphics artist- someone who does graphics! Needless to say, we don't have one working on our diskmag! So if there's any out there.....
MUSICIAN Well, this must be really hard to guess!
LAMER There's a lot of confusion about this one, but at PBHQ we take it to mean someone who makes false claims about their status and things they do in the scene. For example, someone else bragging that they coded the menu interface for our diskmag. Basically another word for a bullshitter! The reason for the confusion about the meaning of "lamer" is that a lot of people in the computer underground (Many of whom could themselves be legitimately termed "lamers"!) just use it as a simple term of abuse!
RIPPER Someone who uses someone elses work. This is not neccessarily a bad thing as long as you credit the person you ripped the work off! For example, using someone else's music in your program and saying somewhere "This great music is by...."
CRACKER A lot of commercial software has protection built into it to stop us ordinary mortals making pirate copies of it! A cracker removes the protection bit. Needless to say, these are people ELSPA don't like very much! These are also people one doesn't seem to see too much of in the PC scene.
SWAPPER Or mailer. This person sends the latest software (Which might or might not be pirate software.) he/she recives to other contacts in the country, who send him/her software in return. A lot of "scene" software is spread by post.
DEMO Guess what- it's short for demonstration! Only what's being demonstrated is the various abilities of coders, graphists, musicians, all stuck together in what is supposed to be an impressive audio/visual show! Yes, you guessed it, it's also used as an ego-trip as well! "Oh look at us, we can do all these amazing things, etc., etc..."
SCROLLER This has been an important part of demos, especially on 16-bit computers- it's basically a constant line of text moving from left to right. As you can guess, it can be used to say anything you want to say! They originally started of as static lines of text, but as the years have went by, there's been every sort of distorting scroller you can imagine- most of them bloody unreadable!
PRECALCULATING Basically, it means making calculations in advance. So what you might think, but this practice can speed up a lot of the nice graphics and sounds effects you get in a demo, since you don't have to bother calculating them at the time! Well, that's all very nice, you might say, but no apparently this is a mortal sin if you're a demo coder! You're supposed to calculate them in real-time if you're supposed to be any good! Well, we at PBHQ think that's a a load of bullshit! Make your routines as fast as you can of course, but the whole bloody point of demos is that they're supposed to look and sound good, so concentrate on that small matter, thank you very much!
MEGADEMO A whole load of demos stuck together on one disk! In some demos there is a big menu to select them with, more often than not, it resembles a shite platform game!
INTRO A demo which is used as the introduction to another program. (eg the introduction to a megademo)
DENTRO This word's used for a single multi-part demo. Why not just use the word "demo" I ask?
CD MENU This can mean two things:
  1. Programs on a CD-ROM disk selectable by a menu. Yes, there are tons of CD-ROM menu, some legitimate (eg clip art, PD games), some not so legitimate (Ahar me hearties!).
  2. "Compacted disk"! On 16-bit machines, program packers were developed which, using clever coding, could compact programs down to a smaller size and still allow them to be run! The result was that sometimes many program could fit onto a disk, so in this case there would be a menu to allow you to select which program you want to run. Most of them would have all the usual bells and whistles ie music, graphics and scrollies! Again, you got some legatimate CD menus and some not-so-legit ones.
CREW A group of people who do stuff on their computer. There's different types of crews eg Demo Crews, Cracking Crews, Lamer Crews, and even Lame Diskmag Crews ie us!
SHOOT-EM-UP A certain type of game, where, as the term suggests, a game player succeeds by shooting anything and everything on the screen! (eg "Space Invaders")
PLATFORM Another type of game where the game player controls a character who moves between various parts of a level which contain various different areas or "platforms" within that level. Famous platform games include "Donky Kong", "Jet Set Willy", "Super Mario Bros" and "Sonic The Hedgehog". As you can guess, this sort of game is very common on console formats!
DOC Short for Document. This usually refers to a text or word- processor file. For example, all PD and sharware utilities usually have doc files containing instructions, and many pirated games contain docs containing instructions and even some cheat codes! There has also been a couple of crews solely involved in producing docs for pirated software, eg Sewer Software and more recently Deviant Docs on Atari computers, and LSD on the Amiga.
DOC DISPLAYER A program which is designed to display text files. This is often used in CD Menus for instructions to games and programs, etc., etc., ... It is also an important part of a diskmag, the bit that displays all the articles! Usually, you can scroll up and down a document, and do things like print it out.
MODEM A nice little bit of computer hardware that allows computers to communicate with each other over the phone line! This has apparently ushered in a "telecommunications revolution", and other tired marketing phrases. The faster the data transfer (baud) rate, the more you can transfer... Apparently, using the amazing DSP chip, the Atari Falcon 030 can do all this using software and a simple adapter cable! (We've yet to see it though!)
BULLETIN BOARD (Or bbs for short.) This is a program designed for computers which have modems, and allows people to call the computer up, and fool about within limits set by the person running the bbs (or sysop). Generally, the sysop allows registered users of the bbs to leave messages for other users, to send (or upload) programs for other users, and to copy (or download) programs which other users have uploaded and the sysop has vetted. (Well, the sysop should if he/she's any good. Don't want any old crap on a bbs!) Very popular with a lot of pirate swappers, who call up pirate bbs' and exchange the latest software!
PHREAKING A quaint little US phrase pertaining to the practice of mucking about with the phone system! The usual thing is to get long phone calls and not be charged with them (As used by a lot of pirate bbs users!), but other tricks can be played. Currently in a bit of a downswing due to the fact that most of the states in the US and a lot of the UK have changed to a new phone system, so most of the old "standard" methods of fooling the phone system have become redundant. However, we can expect new ways to be found....
INTERNET Well, if you don't know what this, why are you currently on it?
UNIX An old multi-tasking operating system which has sort of become the standard for Internet communication, mainly because it's the system that universities and various organisations use in their computers. You can now set up Linux, a version of UNIX on your own home computer, if you have enough hard drive space and enough memory!
USENET A massive message network, which uses the Internet's communication lines and protocols to spread messages and views on literally hundreds of subjects! (eg comp.sys.atari.st is a conference for ST users, and there's also alt.tv.red-dwarf where you can have a good laugh at all the US Unix Wizes who don't know what the fuck "smeghead" means!)
FTP Stands for File Transfer Protocol- basically, an FTP server is the Internet equivalent of the uploading and downloading part of a bbs. Our diskmag has been uploaded to three well- used FTP servers! [Read "The Complete Lamers Guide to FTP" in PB #23 for more details on how to use FTP.] Most major scene stuff is now spread publicy by FTP.
WORLD WIDE WEB Sigh. This is the World Wide Web.
SCENE Well, when we talk about it here, we mean the various networks of users who do all this shit.
